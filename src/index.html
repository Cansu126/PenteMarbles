The Transformation of Marble into Art

Our Marble Types

Pentelic Marble
  • Source: Mount Penteli, north of Athens.
  • It is finely textured and bright white.
  • It has golden colored veins inside.
  • It was used for the Parthenon Temple and other Athenian monuments.
  • Usage: Sculpture and architecture.
Parian Marble
  • Source: Paros Island.
  • It has a bright white color, fine texture and a transparent appearance.
  • It was especially preferred by sculptors because it was of very highquality.
  • Usage: The statue Aphrodite is made of this marble.
Naxian Marble
  • Source: Naxos Island.
  • It is coarser and more durable than Parian marble.
  • It varies between white and gray tones.
  • Usage: Large temples and colossal statues.
Thasian Marble
  • Source:Thassos Island.
  • It is white, similar to Parian marble, and has a fine texture.
  • It was preferred for sculpture making.
  • Usage: Artworks and temples.
Proconnesian Marble
  • Source:Prokonnesos Island.
  • It has a white color, hard and durable structure.
  • Usage: Columns and large structural elements.
Euboean Marble
  • Source: Evia Island.
  • It is in shades of white or light grey.
  • It is hard and durable.
  • Usage: Columns and large building projects.

Masterpieces in Marble: Completed Projects

Stoa of Attalos

Ah, the Stoa of Attalos, an elegant structure in the heart of Athens that accompanies the liveliness of the Agora! Attalos, the generous king of Pergamon, had this stoa built as a gift to the Athenians. With its long columns and double-storey structure, it is both eye-catching and functional. Doric columns on the lower floor and Ionic columns on the upper floor represent elegance. Here, tradesmen line up their shops, philosophers share their thoughts, and people rest in the shade. As a place where commerce, art and conversation meet, the stoa reflects the spirit of the city. The beauty of its stones under the sun attracts people; This place is a meeting point for everyone.

Knossos Palace

Ah, the Palace of Knossos! A fascinating collection of buildings in the heart of Crete, reflecting the splendor of Minoans. Kings lived here and ties were established with the gods. Festivals were held in its large courtyards, and bulls danced in the frescoes painted on its walls. The rooms inside the palace resemble a labyrinth; Every corridor hides a different secret. The stories of the Minotaur also wander in the echoes of these stone walls. In their storehouses are earthen jars of oil, wine, and grain; The palace is the center of both prosperity and order. As the winds of Crete wander through its columns, Knossos is a salute to the creative power of man and the magnificence of nature.

Theatre of Epidaurus

Ah, the Theater of Epidaurus... A magnificent structure rising next to a sacred place dedicated to the God Asclepius, celebrating the harmony of man with nature! The plays staged here are not just entertainment, they are rituals that purify the souls. It is an engineering marvel worthy of the gods, with its perfect acoustics where sounds echo from the stone rows and reach the farthest corners. Every summer, people come from surrounding cities; They find the meaning of life in songs, dances and tragedies. When you look up from the middle steps, the view spanning the surrounding lush green hills is breathtaking. The Theater of Epidaurus is not just a building, it is the place where the heart of a community and the gods beats.

Altar of Pergamon

Ah, the Altar of Pergamon, a sacred work symbolizing the power of the gods and the glory of Pergamon! This huge altar dedicated to Zeus rises into the sky above the city, as if it builds a bridge between the gods and mortals. When you go up the stairs, you will be greeted by fascinating reliefs. This detailed workmanship, which describes the Gigantomachy, that is, the war of the gods with the giants, goes beyond the stone and keeps a legend alive. Not only sacrifices are offered here, but the people of Pergamon express their devotion to the gods. The prayers echoing in the ceremonies mix with the sky with the scent of incense. This altar is the pride of our city and proof that we are blessed by the gods. May the gods protect us all and raise Pergamon even higher!

Athens Agora

Ah, Athens Agora, the heart and soul of Athens! Here you will witness life flowing with all its liveliness. The agora is not only a marketplace, but also a sacred place where ideas collide, laws are debated, and gods are honored. When the sun rises, merchants set up their stalls and display their various wares: olive oil amphorae, fresh fish, soft fabrics and handcrafted products. In one corner, philosophers explain wisdom to their students; Socrates' voice often echoes in the Agora. Temples are places of worship dedicated to our gods. The temple of Hephaestus honors the god's supreme talent for craftsmanship, and our devotion to Athena, the patroness of our city, is felt in every corner. Agora is not just a place, it is the soul of Athens; This is a place where citizens come together, thought and commerce flourish, and freedom breathes.

Miletus City Plan

Miletos is the pride of Ionia, an admirable expression of intelligence and order! The plan of our city is a combination of Gods grace and human wisdom. This plan, shaped by the hands of Hippodamos, proves to everyone that order is superior to chaos. The city was built in a perfect grid pattern. Every street, every square was designed according to a certain size and order. Instead of narrow and complex roads, we have wide avenues and streets that intersect each other at right angles. Thanks to this system, our people find their way easily, and our merchants transport their goods quickly. The center of Miletos, its agora, is the heart of trade and exchange of ideas. Everything is in its place here. Our temples rise with splendor worthy of our gods; Our sacred areas, erected in honor of Poseidon, guide our sailors. Miletos is not just a city, it is a living example of order and harmony. This plan is proof that humans can challenge nature and that mind and art can work together in harmony.
